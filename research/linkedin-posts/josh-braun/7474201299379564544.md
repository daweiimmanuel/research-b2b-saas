Cold email tip. 

Most people don't believe they have a problem.

They believe they're handling the problem.

When a cold email says:

"You're overpaying."
"You're losing money."
"Your broker isn't doing their job."

The reader's instinct is to defend themselves.

Not because they're stubborn.
Because nobody wants to feel incompetent.

The moment someone feels judged, they stop being curious.

That's why this works:

"You probably have a broker keeping an eye on things already."

"Claims change. Adjusters change. The mod doesn't always tell the full story."

You're not blaming anyone.

You're explaining why a gap can exist even when good people are doing good work.

That matters because it lowers resistance.

The prospect doesn't have to choose between:
1. Admitting they're doing something wrong.
2. Rejecting your message.

Instead, they can think:
"Yeah, I can see how that could happen."

That's a much safer thought.
And safer thoughts create curiosity.
Curiosity creates conversations.

The best cold emails don't tell people they're wrong.

They explain why reasonable people can end up with an unexpected outcome. 

That's what makes the message feel helpful instead of threatening.
