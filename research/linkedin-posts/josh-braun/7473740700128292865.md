One of the most useful sales skills has nothing to do with persuasion.

It's called reframing.

A reframe doesn't change the facts.

It changes the meaning.

For example:

"I got ghosted."
→ "I got information."

"I got a no."
→ "I got clarity."

"The prospect wasn't interested." → "The problem wasn't expensive enough."

"They stopped responding to my emails." → "My message isn't compelling right now."

"The decision is taking forever." → "There are stakeholders I haven't identified yet."

As sellers we're taught to change minds.

Elite sellers help people see the same situation through a different lens.

That's why poke the bear questions are so powerful.

Instead of:

"You need to follow up faster."

Try:

"Saw a stat that car buyers usually go with whoever responds first and your odds crater after five minutes. When a lead comes in at 8:17 PM, how long does it typically take before a salesperson reaches out?"

Notice the difference.

One tells.

One invites discovery.

People resist conclusions that are handed to them.

They're much more open to conclusions they arrive at themselves.

A good salesperson doesn't force a new picture into someone's frame.

They simply hand them a different frame and let them look.
