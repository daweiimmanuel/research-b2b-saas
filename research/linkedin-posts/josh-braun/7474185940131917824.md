A lot of sales advice starts with:

"Find a problem."

But here's the thing.

I have a problem with the TV in my guest bedroom.

One pixel is out.

It's annoying.

It's a problem.

And I'm not going to do anything about it.

Why?

Because it has to compete with every other problem in my life.

A broken fence gate.
A cracked garage window.
Paint chipping in the kitchen cabinets. 

The TV problem isn't losing because it isn't a problem.
It's losing because it isn't important enough.
I have limited resources. 
So do you.
So do your prospects. 

As sellers we ask:

"Do you have this problem?"

A better question is:

"Where does this problem rank compared to everything else competing for their time and money right now?"

That's why having a problem isn't enough.

The problem has to win the competition.

You can't talk a #17 priority into becoming a #1 priority.

You can't manufacture urgency.
You can only align with it.
